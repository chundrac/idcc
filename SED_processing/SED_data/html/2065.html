*ngr - herald\n (DUL 623, Watson 2007:97, Kogan 2015:339)\n

\n
\n
\n\n
\n
\n

The origin of Akk.-Ugr. *n\xc4\x81gir- \xe2\x80\x98herald\xe2\x80\x99 is uncertain. Huehnergard\xe2\x80\x99s assertion \xe2\x80\x9cthe word n\xc4\x81giru \xe2\x80\x98herald\xe2\x80\x99 is not attested in any Semitic language other than Akkadian\xe2\x80\x9d (1987a:94) is nevertheless correct (contra J. Sanmart\xc3\xadn).

\n \n Akkadian\n \n

\n \n n\xc4\x81giru - herald\n (CAD N\xe2\x82\x81 115, AHw. 711)\n \n
\n
\n
\n\n
\n

Fem. n\xc4\x81girtu.
\r\nCf. Gez. nagara \xe2\x80\x98to say\xe2\x80\x99 and its Ethiopian cognates (Kogan 2015: 340: a non-attested *nag\xc4\x81ru \xe2\x80\x98to announce\xe2\x80\x99, but nugguru \xe2\x80\x98to denounce\xe2\x80\x99 as well as in its nominal derivates munaggiru \xe2\x80\x98informer\xe2\x80\x99 (CAD M\xe2\x82\x82 198) and taggirtu \xe2\x80\x98denunciation\xe2\x80\x99 (CAD T 38). Hard to separate from Sum. nimgir with the same meaning, and a borrowing from Akk. to Sum. appears more likely than vice versa (Edzard 1981:284\xe2\x80\x92285, Steiner 2003:634, Sassmannshausen 1995:91\xe2\x80\x9292, contra Sommerfeld 2006:64)

\n \n Ugaritic\n \n
\n
\n \n ngr, ngrt - herald\n (DUL 623, Watson 2007:97)\n \n
\n
\n
\n\n
\n

Attested as a divine epithet. The widely accepted interpretation of ngr and ngrt (e.g., Pardee 1997:623) seems superior to *nagg\xc4\x81r- \xe2\x80\x98carpenter\xe2\x80\x99.
