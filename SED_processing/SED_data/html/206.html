*\xe1\xb8\xa5\xe1\xb8\x8fy/*\xe1\xb8\xa5zy - to see\n (Kogan 2015: 394\xe2\x80\x93395)\n

\n
\n
\n\n
\n
\n Level?\r\n\r\nIn spite of the well-known phonological difficulty (Ugr. d presupposes\r\n*\xe1\xb8\x8f in the prototype, as against *z patent in Aramaic and Arabic (One cannot exclude, however, that the root-variant with *\xe1\xb8\x8f is behind \xe1\xb8\xa5\xe1\xb8\x8fw (III) \xe2\x80\x98to face,\r\nto be in front of)), it is very\r\nlikely that all the aforementioned forms have the same etymological background\r\n(cf. DRS 838\xe2\x80\x92839, 854). The dialectal distribution of this root with respect\r\nto PWS *r\xcb\x80y is no less puzzling, particularly in EthS where it seems to be\r\ncompletely absent from the languages of the northern branch (unless one compares Tgr. \xe1\xb8\xa5aza \xe2\x80\x98to seek, to try\xe2\x80\x99, semantically not improbable, but has become\r\nhighly prominent almost throughout the southern one\n

\n \n Phoenician\n \n
\n
\n \n \xe1\xb8\xa5z - to see\n (DNWSI 357\xe2\x80\x92359)\n \n
\n
\n
\n\n
\n This is the only verb with the meaning \xe2\x80\x9cto see\xe2\x80\x9d attested in the corpus, but two out of three extant examples come from KAI 24 where Aramaic influence would be natural (explicitly treated as an Aramaism in Tropper 1993a:278).\n

\n
\n
\n\n
\n The word is an almost exclusively poetic verb whose presence in the Biblical corpus may be partly due to Aramaic influence (Wagner 1966:54).\n
