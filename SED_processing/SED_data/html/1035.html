*\xe1\xb8\xabatan- - son-in-law, groom\n (Kogan 2011: 236; Kogan 2015: 191)\n

\n
\n
\n\n
\n
\n occasionally also \xe2\x80\x98father-in-law\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98brother-in-law\xe2\x80\x99\r\n Connection with the verbal root \xe1\xb8\xabtn \xe2\x80\x98to circumcise,\xe2\x80\x99 completely isolated in Arabic (Lane 703), is\r\nwidely accepted (HALOT 364\xe2\x80\x92365), but difficult to prove (Kogan 2015: 191)\n

\n \n Akkadian\n \n
\n
\n \n \xe1\xb8\xabatanu - relative by marriage (son-in-law, brother-in-law, bridegroom)\n (CAD \xe1\xb8\xaa 148, AHw. 335)\n \n
\n
\n
\n\n
\n Probably a WS loanword (with Goetze 1947:246\xe2\x80\x92247). This hypothesis is attractive in view of the fact that Akk. emu was clearly polysemic (\xe2\x80\x9cfatherin-law\xe2\x80\x9d/\xe2\x80\x9cson-in-law\xe2\x80\x9d) in OB and OA, whereas \xe1\xb8\xabatanu is only sparsely attested in early periods (Kogan 2014:104\xe2\x80\x92105). Also the morphological shape of the Akkadian word (non-syncopated *a in the second syllable) is unusual.\n
