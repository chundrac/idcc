\n \n Hebrew\n \n
\n
\n \n \xe1\xb8\xb3\xe1\xb9\xadl - to kill\n (HALOT 1092)\n \n
\n
\n
\n\n
\n Hbr. \xe1\xb8\xb3\xe1\xb9\xadl is borrowed from Aramaic (see Wagner 1966:100\xe2\x80\x92101 and Kogan 2015:111-112)\n

\n \n Samalian\n \n
\n
\n \n \xe1\xb8\xb3tl - to kill\n (DNWSI 1006)\n \n
\n
\n
\n\n
\n \xe1\xb8\xb3tl \xe2\x80\x98to kill\xe2\x80\x99 is attested only once in Samalian (KAI 215:8) in a derived nominal form \xe1\xb8\xb3tylt (byt \xe1\xb8\xb3tylt, presumably \xe2\x80\x98Haus der/f\xc3\xbcr die get\xc3\xb6teten Frauen,\xe2\x80\x99 Tropper 1993a:115).\n

\n \n Old Aramaic\n \n
\n
\n \n \xe1\xb8\xb3tl - to kill\n (DNWSI 1006)\n \n
\n
\n
\n\n
\n Forms with non-emphatic \xe1\xb9\xad cease to be attested in post-OArm. dialects. A well-known exception is yk\xe1\xb9\xadlwk in KAI 225:11, whose non-emphatic k is highly unusual and remains to be explained.\n
