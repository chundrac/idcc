\n \n PS\n \n
\n
\n \n *dwr - to go around, to turn\n (EDA II)\n \n
\n
\n
\n\n
\n

In various Semitic languages, PS *dwr \xe2\x80\x98to go round\xe2\x80\x99 acquired the meaning \xe2\x80\x98to stay permanently in one place, to dwell\xe2\x80\x99. A variant root *dhr is attested in some WS languages.

\n \n Akkadian\n \n
\n
\n \n d\xc5\xabru - (city) wall\n (AHw. 178, CAD D 192)\n \n
\n
\n
\n\n
\n

See also d\xc5\xabr\xc4\x81ni\xc5\xa1 \xe2\x80\x98over the wall\xe2\x80\x99 (AHw. 177, CAD D 190).\xc2\xa0The underlying semantic development is, most probably, from \xe2\x80\x98what surrounds, encircles\xe2\x80\x99 (Marrassini 1971, 49f.). A derivation from the secondary meaning \xe2\x80\x98to dwell (\xe2\x80\x99(walled) dwelling space\xe2\x80\x99) is not to be ruled out completely (if accepted, at least some of the WS nominal derivations with the meaning \xe2\x80\x9cdwelling\xe2\x80\x9d listed here may be directly related to Akk.\xc2\xa0d\xc5\xabru). Still another semantic derivation, discussed in Buck 1949, 473 for Germanic words for \xe2\x80\x9cwall\xe2\x80\x9d (Gothic\xc2\xa0-waddjus, German\xc2\xa0Wand), is from \xe2\x80\x9cto turn, to plait\xe2\x80\x9d as referring to a wall of wattle-work.
