PS
\n
\n

*wkl - to (en)trust, to rely\n (DRS 539, Kogan 2015:239, EDA II)\n

\n
\n
\n\n
\n
\n

PNWS *ykl \xe2\x80\x98to be able\xe2\x80\x99 may be related.

\r\n\r\n

According to a broad consensus summarised in Kouwenberg 2010a, 454\xc2\xa0and Kogan 2015, 101, Akk.\xc2\xa0tak\xc4\x81lu\xc2\xa0is a diachronically secondary verb going back\xc2\xa0to PS *wkl\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cto trust, to rely\xe2\x80\x9d.\xc2\xa0The same type of secondary root has been independently produced in Arabic and Aramaic. As reasonably argued by Kaufman 1974, 106, the high proliferation of *tkl in Aramaic (notably, in opposition to its modest presence in Arabic) may be due to Akkadian influence on Aramaic or even to a direct borrowing from Akkadian.

\n \n PES\n \n
\n
\n \n *tkl - to fix, to fasten, to plant\n \n
\n
\n
\n\n
\n

Noeldeke 1886, 726 tentatively connected the Akkadian\xe2\x80\x93Aramaic isogloss *tkl (considered to be a secondary derivation from PS *wkl) with PES *tkl\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cto fix, to fasten, to plant\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0. This comparison, implying that Ethiopian preserves the primary meaning \xe2\x80\x9cto fix, to be well-fixed, reliable\xe2\x80\x9d and undermining the derivation from *w-k-l, is discussed and rejected by Leslau in CDG 573.
